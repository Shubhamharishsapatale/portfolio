'Ikshura': A Robotic Manipulator for Sugarcane Harvesting

MSc Dissertation

For my MSc dissertation in Robotics at the University of Sheffield, I designed and evaluated 'Ikshura' a robotic manipulator aimed at improving sugarcane harvesting through automation. The project addressed challenges like labour shortages, crop damage, and energy inefficiency by developing a lightweight, precision-controlled end-effector.

I created a fully parametric 3D model in Fusion 360, featuring a dual-gripper mechanism for stalk stabilization and a NEMA17 motor-driven cutting blade. Finite Element Analysis using ANSYS revealed that a 5 mm blade design performed significantly better under simulated cutting stress, compared to a thinner 2 mm version.

While the design showed promise in strength and control, future improvements include refining blade geometry, incorporating AI-driven perception, and testing under real field conditions to account for environmental variability.

SLS Robot: Autonomous Navigation & Robotic Arm Control

Led a three-member team in developing the SLS Robot. A mobile platform with autonomous navigation and a 2-DOF robotic arm capable of whiteboard drawing. From concept to execution, we built the robot entirely in-house, designing the chassis and arm in Fusion 360 and fabricating components using laser-cut acrylic and 3D printing.

The system was powered by an Arduino Mega, integrating ultrasonic sensors, DC motors with encoders, and servo motors for precise actuation. Implemented inverse kinematics for the arm to accurately replicate drawings and developed sensor-based logic for autonomous movement, including wall-following and obstacle avoidance.

To ensure consistent marker contact, we designed a spring-loaded end-effector, merging mechanical precision with intelligent control in a compact, task-oriented robot.

Autonomous Exploration with TurtleBot3 Using ROS and SLAM

In a team of four, I helped develop and deploy a ROS-based system that enabled the TurtleBot3 Waffle to autonomously explore and map a bounded arena using SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping). Starting in simulation, we implemented key behaviours like obstacle avoidance with LIDAR data, wall-following, and randomized movement.

These capabilities were governed by a Finite State Machine (FSM) to allow seamless switching between navigation modes. After successful virtual testing, we transitioned to real-world deployment, fine-tuning parameters for reliable operation.

Our system generated live maps as the robot explored, demonstrating robust autonomy and intelligent spatial awareness.

Predicting Chemical Biodegradability Using Machine Learning

Developed a logistic regression model to classify chemical compounds as biodegradable or non-biodegradable using QSAR (Quantitative Structure–Activity Relationship) data. Working with a dataset of 1,055 compounds described by 41 structural and physicochemical features, applied Local Outlier Factor to clean the data, standardized the inputs, and used L2 regularization to improve model generalization.

Hyperparameters were fine-tuned for optimal performance, achieving an accuracy of 86.7%. The model's predictive strength was further validated with an ROC curve, yielding an AUC score of 0.82—demonstrating strong potential for real-world applications in environmental impact assessment.

Optimizing Electron Beam Melting Using Reinforcement Learning

Led a five-member team in applying reinforcement learning to optimize the Electron Beam Melting (EBM) process in additive manufacturing. Our aim was to dynamically control the electron beam power to maintain a consistent melt-pool temperature crucial for part quality while minimizing energy consumption.

We implemented a Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) algorithm and structured the control system across three spatial levels: layer, track, and point. Through precise reward engineering and strategic sampling methods like Full Factorial and Latin Hypercube Sampling, we trained an RL agent that significantly improved thermal stability and reduced energy use by 15%.

The point-level control stage delivered the highest precision, showcasing the potential of intelligent control in high-performance manufacturing.

Flow Boiling Heat Transfer Enhancement Using Perforated Twisted Tapes

Undergrad Dissertation

For our Mechanical Engineering dissertation, my team of three conducted an experimental study to enhance flow boiling heat transfer of refrigerant R404A using custom-designed perforated twisted tape inserts in a horizontal tube. These inserts, with twist ratios of 8 and 10, served as passive turbulence promoters aimed at improving the thermal efficiency of low-GWP, zero-ODP refrigerants.

We built a dedicated test rig featuring a custom evaporator section equipped with thermocouples and pressure transducers for accurate data collection. The results demonstrated a marked improvement in heat transfer performance, particularly in the lower vapor quality range (10%–50%), with only a slight increase in pressure drop, highlighting a practical and eco-conscious method to boost thermal system efficiency.
